Heat Sink-Enhanced TEG

Converting waste heat into electricity using the Seebeck effect.

Abstract: In this project, energy is generated by converting waste heat into electricity. The primary objective was the design of a prototype capable of transforming heat into electrical energy using thermoelectric modules (Peltier).

🔥 The Problem

Current energy systems suffer from reduced efficiency and increasing operational costs. A significant portion of energy in domestic/industrial sectors is lost as Waste Heat, which remains an untapped resource causing environmental harm.

❄ The Solution

A highly scalable, eco-friendly heat recovery system simulating a domestic iron. It uses a temperature difference (hot iron vs. recycled fan/ice) to generate clean electricity via Peltier modules, exceeding the 150J target to produce 2700J.

System Architecture

Interactive overview of the prototype's physical construction and materials. Select a component to view its details.

🔥

Electric Iron (Hot Side)

Reaches temperatures of approximately 180-200°C. Simulates domestic and industrial waste heat.

❄

Recycled Fan + Ice/Salt

A computer fan combined with a mixture of ice and salt to achieve and maintain a temperature of 0°C. The salt helps the ice last longer.

⚡

Thermoelectric Modules

Placed between the hot side and cold side. Utilizes the Seebeck Effect to convert the temperature difference directly into voltage.

🧱

Aluminum Plates & Compound

Aluminum plates distribute heat evenly to prevent module damage. Thermal compound (heat sink paste) fills gaps to drastically improve thermal transfer efficiency.

Mathematical Analysis

Detailed calculations proving the efficiency and success of the prototype based on physics principles.

📚 Governing Formulas

  • Power Formula:
    P = V² / R
  • Energy Formula:
    E = P × t
  • Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction:
    Q = (k × A × ΔT) / L
  • Constants: Resistance (R) = 1 Ω, Time (t) = 300s, Energy Input (Qin) = 10,800 J.

➀ Trial 1 Evaluation

P = (1.8)² / 1 = 3.24 W
E = 3.24 × 300 = 972 J
Efficiency = (972 / 10800) × 100 = 9%

➁ Trial 2 Evaluation

P = (2.0)² / 1 = 4.0 W
E = 4.0 × 300 = 1200 J
Efficiency = (1200 / 10800) × 100 = 11%

➂ Trial 3 Evaluation (Final)

P = (3.0)² / 1 = 9.0 W
E = 9.0 × 300 = 2700 J
Efficiency = (2700 / 10800) × 100 = 25%

Fourier's Law Application

Calculating the heat transfer rate through the materials properties (ΔT = 90°C - 22.5°C = 67.5°C):

k = 1, A = 0.04 × 0.04 = 0.0016 m², L = 0.003 m

Q = (1 × 0.0016 × 67.5) / 0.003 = 36 W
Energy Input (Qin) = 36 W × 300 s = 10,800 J
