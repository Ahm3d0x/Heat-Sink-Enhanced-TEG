The Problem

Egypt faces rising energy demand and fossil fuel dependence. A significant portion of industrial and domestic energy is lost as Waste Heat — an invisible, untapped resource causing environmental harm and rising costs.

🌱

The Solution

A compact Peltier-based TEG system sandwiched between a 180–200°C iron and a 0°C ice/salt cold sink. Using the Seebeck Effect across an optimized ΔT > 150°C, it produced up to 2700 J from pure waste heat.

Introduction & Context

Prior solutions, Egypt's Grand Challenge, and the project's approach.

Egypt's Grand Challenge

Egypt aims to improve use of alternative energies, reduce pollution, adapt to climate change, and use recycled materials given high reliance on electricity in daily life (Enerdata, 2026). Current energy systems suffer from reduced efficiency and increasing operational costs, making integrated solutions critical.

Prior Solutions Analyzed

Road Thermoelectric Generator System

Bełchatów Coal Power Station

✅ HS-TEG Advantage

The HS-TEG addresses both prior solutions' shortcomings: it is cost-effective (645 EGP total), eco-friendly using recycled materials (computer fan, heat sink), produces no emissions, and is highly scalable. It demonstrates that waste heat from everyday appliances like irons can power small devices sustainably.

⚡ The Seebeck Effect

When two dissimilar semiconductors (P-type and N-type materials inside the Peltier module) are subjected to a temperature difference, charge carriers diffuse from the hot side to the cold side, creating a measurable voltage difference. This is the fundamental physical principle powering the HS-TEG.

Experimental Test Plan

Three iterative trials with documented outcomes and video evidence.

Trial 1

Baseline Setup

1.8V | 3.24W | 972J | Eff: 9%

Two Peltier modules connected in series. Heat gun applied to aluminum plate. Excessive temperature caused one module to burn due to thermal overload — experiment continued with a single module.
ΔT = 90°C − 22.5°C = 67.5°C

▶
Trial 2

+ Thermal Compound

2.0V | 4.0W | 1200J | Eff: 11%

Heat sink compound applied beneath the Peltier module. This eliminated microscopic air gaps, drastically improving thermal transfer and protecting the module from direct heat. Energy improved from 972 J to 1200 J (+23%).

▶
Trial 3 — Best

+ Ice & Salt Cooling

3.0V | 9.0W | 2700J | Eff: 25%

Ice and salt mixture introduced alongside the fan, forcing the cold side to 0°C. Salt lowers the freezing point of water, sustaining the temperature longer. Achieved ΔT > 150°C. Target of 150 J exceeded by 18×, improvement target of 10% exceeded at 25%.

▶

⚠️ Challenges & Negative Outcomes

  • •One Peltier module was damaged (burned) during Trial 1 due to thermal overload when two modules were connected in series — experiment continued with a single module.
  • •High heat intensity from the iron posed a risk of melting plastic wiring and insulation — highlighting the need for heat-resistant wiring in future applications.

Future Recommendations

Suggested improvements to enhance efficiency, durability, and scalability.

🧊

Dry Ice for Cooling

Using dry ice provides stronger cooling without producing liquid water, preventing water from reaching the fan or electrical components. It creates a larger temperature difference, improving Peltier module efficiency significantly.

⚡

Multiple Peltier Modules

Increasing the number of Peltier modules connected in series increases output voltage. This improves measurability of generated energy and enhances the efficiency of thermal-to-electrical conversion. Requires heat-resistant wiring.

🔋

Rechargeable Battery Storage

Storing generated electrical energy in a rechargeable battery prevents energy loss and makes the system more practical. The stored energy can power small devices or low-power applications on demand.

🌀

Active Heat Sink + Fan

A heat sink combined with a fan on the hot side improves heat dissipation, maintaining a stable temperature difference between both sides and increasing efficiency of electrical energy generation over extended periods.
